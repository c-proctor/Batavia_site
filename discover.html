DISCOVER THE BATAVIA
Barbarism & Brutality

On the 28 October, 1628 the VOC (Dutch East India Company) ship Batavia left Texel in The Netherlands bound for Batavia, the company's headquarters in the East. The ship was under the command of Francisco Pelsaert and was carrying about 300 passengers, including women and children.

Because of a personal grievance against Pelsaert, the Master, Adrian Jacobz, together with the Supercargo, Jeronimus Cornelisz, plotted a mutiny. Their plan was to slip away from the fleet after leaving the Cape of Good Hope, seize the Batavia and then pirate other richly laden ships bound for the Indies.

The Portico Fresco

During the 1972-76 excavation of Batavia, divers raised 137 shaped sandstone building blocks weighing a total of 37 tonnes. Like a giant jigsaw, they fitted together to form a magnificent portico façade.

Clues to the reason why the portico was on board Batavia have been found in the journal of Pieter van de Broeke, published in 1634. This work contained a number of woodcuts, one of which showed the town and castle of Batavia (Jakarta). The woodcut, engraved by Adriaen J. Matham of Haarlem, shows the Waterpoort or sea-gate of the castle incomplete, with scaffolding and a ladder in the place of an actual gateway.
